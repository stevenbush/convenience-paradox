In this project, I also aim to incorporate the following reflections of my own:
1. As AI capabilities continue to advance, they will inevitably be applied—gradually but surely—to fields related to agent-based modeling (ABM). However, the interpretability of AI models is a critical factor that must be addressed; specifically, in domains such as social modeling, it is imperative to ensure the interpretability of both the underlying model principles and the resulting simulation outcomes. We must consider how to mitigate the inherent limitations of the foundational models themselves—such as biases or the tendency to generate "average personas"—which can easily lead to simulation results that lack representativeness. Issues such as the following are absolutely essential to consider:
1) The Conflict Between "White Box" and "Black Box" (The Transparency Problem)
Traditional ABM operates as a "white box": Scientists require explicit knowledge of *why* an agent (or "little person") decides to buy a house (e.g., because a specific formula dictates that A=B). This level of transparency serves as the bedrock of scientific inquiry, facilitating reproducibility and enabling the explanation of causal relationships.
LLMs, conversely, function as "black boxes": They generate content based on probabilities. If you were to ask an LLM *why* it decided to buy a house, it might provide a seemingly plausible rationale; however, the actual computational process underlying that decision remains invisible—a mere aggregation of statistical weights. If LLMs were to directly replace traditional modeling techniques, the resulting simulation outcomes would become unverifiable and, consequently, untrustworthy.
2) The Conflict Between "Logic" and "Patterns" (The Rigor Problem)
Traditional ABM relies on "symbolic logic": It possesses the capacity to process extreme or unprecedented scenarios (i.e., counterfactual reasoning).
LLMs, conversely, are "data-driven": They excel at mimicking the "common sense" and patterns embedded within their training data. If a social system were to encounter an unprecedented crisis—a situation never before observed in its training data—an LLM might either "hallucinate" (generate nonsensical outputs) or produce merely mediocre, uninsightful predictions.

2. Consequently, data visualization plays a critical role in simulations; specifically, appropriate visualization techniques are essential for effectively "keeping humans in the loop." The primary objective is to make the simulation process more interactive and insightful. We deal with vast quantities of multi-dimensional simulation data, and without effective visualization and interaction capabilities, it is easy to overlook the underlying narrative within that data. By keeping humans in the loop, we can delve deeper into the analysis while simultaneously ensuring that the simulation results remain both interpretable and trustworthy.

3. Emerging technologies—such as Large Language Models (LLMs)—should be integrated in an appropriate manner throughout the entire lifecycle of Agent-Based Modeling (ABM) to enhance the overall capabilities of the modeling and simulation process. In addition to directly augmenting the capabilities of the agents themselves, future applications for LLMs may include: data processing, data preparation, data digitization support, and enhancing data insights through visualization, among other areas.
